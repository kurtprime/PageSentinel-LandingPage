> **TL;DR:** Website change monitoring automatically detects content, visual, and structural changes on your pages — going far beyond uptime monitoring's "is the server alive?" check. For agencies, ecommerce teams, and compliance departments, it catches broken layouts, pricing errors, and injected code before customers find them.

If you manage websites for clients — whether you're an agency, a freelancer, or an in-house team — you've probably experienced this: a site looks fine at first glance (it loads, it responds), but something is wrong. A layout broke. A pricing table updated incorrectly. A redirect quietly started sending visitors somewhere else.

Traditional uptime monitoring would tell you the site is "up." And technically, it is. But "up" doesn't mean "working correctly."

## What is website change monitoring?

Website change monitoring is the practice of automatically tracking and detecting changes to web pages — not just whether they respond to HTTP requests, but whether their **content, structure, and appearance** have changed in meaningful ways.

A website change monitor captures:

- **Visual changes:** Layout breaks, hidden sections, CSS regressions, design inconsistencies
- **Content changes:** Text edits, pricing updates, policy modifications, new or removed content
- **Structural changes:** HTML modifications, injected scripts, redirect rules, form changes

## Why uptime monitoring isn't enough

Uptime monitors check one thing: "Did the server respond with a 200 OK?" That's it.

Here's what uptime monitoring **won't** catch:

- A checkout form that broke after a theme update (server still returns 200)
- Spam links injected into your footer (server still returns 200)
- A pricing page where someone accidentally changed $29 to $19 (server still returns 200)
- A redirect that silently sends your homepage visitors elsewhere (server still returns 200)

The gap between "site is up" and "site is correct" is where most problems hide — and where change monitoring steps in.

## Who needs website change monitoring?

**Agencies** managing 20-200+ client sites need to know about breakages before clients call. A single plugin update can cascade across multiple sites.

**Ecommerce teams** need to know if checkout pages, pricing tables, or product listings change unexpectedly. Every hour a broken checkout goes undetected is lost revenue.

**Compliance teams** need audit trails for policy pages, terms of service, and regulatory content. Unauthorized edits can have legal consequences.

**Site owners** who depend on their website for leads and revenue need peace of mind that their pages haven't changed without their knowledge.
